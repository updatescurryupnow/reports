Ordering pattern — what to control from the store's own order sheet (P6 invoices)

P6: 78 invoices, $47.3K. Vendors are Sysco ($13.2K over 12 orders), Modesto ($8.5K/12), Viks ($6.4K/8), APP ($5.6K/4), SJ Distributors ($4.3K/10).

What the ordering data shows

  • APP is being ordered in large lumps rather than right-sized drops. P6 had 4 APP orders ranging $620 to $2,267, with the $2,267 single drop on 05/18 well above the $350 minimum. Order APP as often as you need through the week, but keep each order at or above the $350 minimum to avoid delivery fees. The point is to order only what is needed each time, not to pre-buy a week of product in one large drop.
  • Sysco is your biggest line ($13.2K) and already comes frequently, but the drops are lumpy (up to $2,181 in one order). Since San Jose can take deliveries throughout the week, split Sysco into smaller, more frequent orders tied to that day's need rather than one big stock-up.
  • Instacart ($1,896 across 9 small buys) and one-off Autochlor ($1,064) are filling gaps that planned ordering should cover. Instacart should be the emergency exception, not a weekly habit.

What to improve

  • Order to need, not to the week. San Jose can receive deliveries every day, so place smaller orders across the week against actual prep needs instead of one large stock-up. This is the single biggest control lever here.
  • Order APP multiple times through the week as needed, keeping each order at or above the $350 minimum to avoid delivery fees. This replaces the lumpy $620 to $2,267 swings with right-sized, need-based drops.
  • Cap Sysco single drops. Break the big Sysco orders into two or three smaller mid-week deliveries so inventory does not sit.
  • Make Instacart emergency-only. Track every Instacart buy; if a SKU shows up there twice, add it to the planned vendor order.
  • Modesto and SJ Distributors can both deliver multiple times a week, so small frequent orders from them are good budget control. Order only what is needed each drop; no need to consolidate or stock up on these two.

Findings for Anmol

1
Prime cost is the headline.
Budget tracker prime ran 57.5% (P4), 56.1% (P5), 59.7% (P6) against a 51% goal, and the booked P&L was worse in P4 at 63.2% actual, a +5.7 point gap to tracker. The split is roughly even between food and labor, but labor is the part you control fastest because of overtime.
2
Overtime is the single biggest lever.
San Jose has a zero-OT allowance and went over it every one of the 13 weeks, averaging about 23 OT hours a week and peaking at 47.9 in one week. That OT is what pushes labor from the low-30s toward 37-40% in the worst weeks. Eliminating it is the largest single labor recovery available anywhere in the network.
3
Guest experience is a fulfillment problem, not a food problem.
Ovation comments include orders that never arrived with the restaurant not answering the phone, wrong bowls, missing chutneys, and undercooked vindaloo and paneer. The food is not the issue; the handoff and the phone are. San Jose is also the response-loop laggard at 40% response rate and 17h 8m response time while every other store improved.
4
Catering recovered and must stay recovered.
After a P4 missing-dishes 1-star, San Jose posted two clean 5-stars in P6 once the pre-dispatch dish-count discipline went in. Keep it mandatory. Separately, the long Zanker to Fremont route (25-35 min) is a structural delivery risk to watch on big buffets, though the P6 Nextracker orders delivered fine.

Anmol's 4-week plan to get the budget under control

Prime from 59.7% (P6) toward 53% by end of the four weeks, driven mostly by removing OT and tightening ordering.
Week 1
Kill the overtime
Rebuild the schedule from a zero-OT baseline. Map every shift against forecasted sales by daypart, cut the overlap on slow dayparts, and set a hard rule that no one crosses into OT without your written approval. Post the daily labor target and check actual vs target every morning.
Week 2
Lock the order-accuracy SOP
Mark each item KDS-done only when it is made correctly, write the item name on every box, and check it off on the receipt while confirming the full order is complete before handoff. Assign a named per-shift phone-and-recovery owner so no guest call goes unanswered. Answer the open Ovation and review backlog within 24 hours.
Week 3
Tighten ordering to sales
Set the weekly order guide to forecasted sales, not last week's habit. Do a mid-week inventory check and adjust the second order down if sales are tracking soft. Target food/COGS toward 25% or below. Add a doneness check at the pass for paneer and vindaloo.
Week 4
Hold and verify
Re-audit the full week: OT at zero, labor under 32%, food under 25%, prime moving toward 53%. Confirm the catering dish-count and Fremont dispatch buffer are being followed. Review the four-week trend with the team and set P7 targets.
